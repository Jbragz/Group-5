Glimpse about Public Administration

Public administration encompasses various administrative services that help strengthen and support societies through nonprofit, local, state, and federal governmental roles. It covers labor relations and ethics, legislative relations, public policy management, financial management, and public budgetary processes. It is also a subfield of political science that studies policy processes and the structures, functions, and behavior of public institutions and their relationships with broader society. The study and application of public administration are founded on the principle that the proper functioning of an organization or institution relies on effective management. Public administration encompasses the execution, oversight, and management of government policies and the management of public affairs. The field involves the organization, operation, and strategic coordination of bureaucratic structures in the public sector. Public administrators play a significant role in devising and executing policies, managing shared resources, and ensuring the efficient functioning of government agencies and programs.

The development of the public administration model dating from the mid-1800s to the early 1900s was influenced primarily by Weber's theory of bureaucracy, Northcote and Trevelyan's recommendations relating to the establishment of a professional civil service in Britain, and Woodrow Wilson's ideas in the United States for the separation of policy from administration (Hughes, 1994). Other managerial theories and concepts over the years have been relevant, including Taylor's scientific management and Simon's rational decision-making. Since the 1980s, the traditional model of public administration has been largely rejected by governments in favor of a more focused managerialist model based on private-sector practice, within the context of a market-based economic model of public organization. In more recent years, there have been attempts to balance the economic focus with a renewed emphasis on public organizations creating public value. This contribution will first examine the traditional model of public administration, including its conceptual and theoretical bases, and how this has affected organizational aspects. Second, it will explore how and why there has been a paradigm shift from public administration to public management. Third, it will consider future trends.

Public administration is the term used to define the formal procedural and organizational arrangements under which public employees serve a government by implementing and advising on policy and managing resources. Organizational aspects refer to both the overall structures as well as the relationships that occur within public administrations. This could include: the organizations that make up a civil service, sometimes referred to as the machinery of government; internal organizational arrangements; and/or organizational behavior.

Overview of Public Administration Course

If you're considering a career in public administration and thinking about taking a course in the field, you're likely on a promising path toward a role that can be both impactful and rewarding. Here's a comprehensive overview of what you might expect from a public administration course and the career opportunities it can lead to:

1. Curriculum Content:

  • Foundational Topics: Introduction to public administration, public policy, and management principles.
  • Specialized Subjects: Public finance, human resources management, ethics in public service, and organizational behavior.
  • Skills Development: Leadership, strategic planning, budgeting, and policy analysis.
  • Practical Applications: Case studies, internships, and capstone projects that provide real-world experience.

2. Learning Outcomes:

  • Knowledge Acquisition: Understanding the structure and function of government agencies, non-profits, and other public sector entities.
  • Skill Building: Developing analytical, managerial, and communication skills necessary for effective public service.
  • Ethical Consideration: Emphasizing ethical decision-making and public accountability.

3. Course Formats:

  • Degree Programs: Bachelor's, Master's (e.g., Master of Public Administration - MPA), and Doctorate (e.g., Doctor of Public Administration - DPA).
  • Certification Programs: Shorter, specialized courses for professional development.
  • Online vs. In-Person: Many institutions offer flexible online programs alongside traditional in-person classes.

Career Opportunities in Public Administration

1. Government Positions:

  • Local, State, and Federal Government: Roles in policy development, urban planning, public health administration, and public safety.
  • Civil Service: Jobs that support the implementation and management of government programs and services.

2. Non-Profit Sector:

  • Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs): Positions in program management, fundraising, and advocacy.
  • Community Organizations: Roles focused on social services, community development, and grassroots organizing.

3. International Organizations:

  • United Nations, World Bank, and Other International Bodies: Careers in international development, policy advising, and program implementation.

4. Private Sector:

  • Consulting Firms: Advising public sector clients on efficiency, strategy, and policy.
  • Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR): Managing and developing CSR programs for private companies.

Skills and Competencies

  • Analytical Skills: Ability to analyze policies, interpret data, and evaluate program effectiveness.
  • Leadership and Management: Leading teams, managing budgets, and strategic planning.
  • Communication: Effective writing and speaking skills for policy advocacy, public relations, and stakeholder engagement.
  • Ethical Judgment: Strong sense of ethics and integrity in decision-making processes.

Benefits of a Career in Public Administration

  • Impactful Work: Directly contributing to societal welfare and public good.
  • Job Stability: Public sector jobs often offer more stability compared to some private sector positions.
  • Career Advancement: Opportunities for growth into higher management and executive roles.
  • Diverse Opportunities: Wide range of sectors and roles to choose from, allowing for career flexibility.

Conclusion

A course in public administration can open doors to a variety of career paths that are both challenging and fulfilling. Whether you aim to work in government, non-profits, international organizations, or the private sector, the skills and knowledge gained from such a course are highly transferable and valued. As you move forward, consider the specific areas of public administration that most interest you and seek out programs that offer robust training and real-world applications in those areas.

Public Administration: The Ethical Challenges

Public administrators may face numerous ethical challenges in upholding and enforcing the law, controlling performance, or facilitating productive communication. This framework emphasizes the sense of justice and equal support for each community member, which is achieved and perpetuated by mutual accountability and care (Fischer, 2010).

The foundational ideas for governance are often sourced from ancient societies’ moral and fact-based principles. Public administrators are expected to make objective decisions (factually correct), but the decisions are not regulated in the same way (O’Reilly, 2011). To demonstrate this issue, a case by van Steden (2020) identifies a lack of ethical approach incorporation, specifically, of integrity, in governance. Exemplary creations, whether in engineering or pottery, typically function as a public testament to one’s work quality, which propagates integrity, consistency, and reliability. Professional craftsmen’s work ethic historically relies on principles outlined by Aristotle: “praxis,” or between-colleagues knowledge sharing, and “phronesis,” which is simultaneously practical wisdom and virtue (van Steden, 2020, p. 239). As van Steden (2020) contends, governance should embrace the ethical obligation to go beyond the bare minimum and achieve professional excellence by continuously reflecting on and refining the process.

Other challenges of public administration ethics may similarly be studied through a prism of ancient literature. Marini (1992) explores the lessons for governance drawn from the works of Sophocles, specifically Antigone. By addressing the challenges facing different characters, various learning points are created, including the problem of conscience in governance (Marini, 1992). The challenge of conscience, to which the play is arguably dedicated, directs public administrative ethics to examine the established rules. However, solely trusting in personal morality does not lead to effective governance. Instead, Marini (1992) suggests that professional administrative ethics should reference conscience coupled with the understanding of the rules and complexities of the managed community. Therefore, the question of morality and integrity is raised and addressed again, this time contextualizing personal values and joining them with professional practices.

Furthermore, the digital era has brought unprecedented challenges to governance as information and communication technology (ICT) has overturned the dynamics of organizational structures. The case study by Emina (2020) evaluates ethical perspectives of digital public administration of virtual societies. For instance, although helpful organizationally, the increasing lack of users’ data confidentiality leads to an eventual breach of trust (Emina, 2020). In using ICT-powered systems, public administrators must be guided by moral and ethical principles to avoid explicit privacy violations (Emina, 2020). This case may benefit from relying on the major principles set out by the biblical/covenantal approach like empowerment of individuals and, ultimately, mutual respect, including respect for online privacy.

Another ethical challenge in public administration concerns the bureaucratic system. One of the notorious guiding concepts in public administration is ‘public interest,’ which allows executing laws “in the public interest, convenience, or necessity” (Shafritz & Hyde, 2017, p. 103). However, a question arises on the ethical implication of justifying any action in the public interest. For instance, a study of torture ethics by Einolf (2016) ponders to which extent the utilitarian approach (means justified by ends) should be referenced in justifying such action. The biblical framework highlights the importance of respecting individuals in the community, aligning closely with deontological ethics, which states that the intent and not consequences determine the moral value of an act (Einolf, 2016). Public administrators are encouraged to evaluate their intent and the virtue it holds before making a governing decision. Overall, virtue, respect, and integrity have been highlighted repeatedly throughout this work as some of public administrators’ major guiding ethical principles.

Diving deep into these two faces: Working in public sectors and working in private sectors.

The primary difference between public- and private-sector jobs is that public-sector jobs are generally within a government agency, while private-sector jobs are those where employees work for non-governmental agencies. This includes jobs within individual businesses and other types of company organizations.

Public Sector:

The part of the economy governed and funded by federal, state, or local government units.

Examples of public-sector employment areas:

Health and Care
Teaching
Emergency services
Armed forces
Civil service
City councils

There are several advantages to working in the public sector, including job stability and the various high-quality benefits packages available. These include excellent retirement benefits and favorable insurance policies. Public employees typically enjoy better job security than private employees, except during major budget cuts. Also, many government positions are permanent appointments once a probationary period has been met. After this period has passed, an employee is unlikely to be laid off.

Private Sector:

The part of the economy comprising companies and organizations not controlled by the government.

Examples of private-sector employment areas:

Financial services
Law firms
Estate agents
Newspapers or magazines
Veterinarians
Aviation
Hospitality

Working in the private sector traditionally outweighs the benefits of working in the public sector. Private sector employment allows greater fluidity if moving from one job to another. Further still, for those individuals remaining within the same company, it is far easier to quickly move up within a company, as these decisions are made within the company rather than based on central rules and regulations stipulated by the government.

Significance of Knowledge Management and ICT in Public Administration

Knowledge Management and ICT in Public Administration is designed to introduce students to the ways that information technology affects governmental functions, democratic processes, and public programs. Topics covered include e-democracy, e-governance, e-government, e-procurement, and information security and privacy.

Course Objectives

  1. Develop a basic knowledge of the impact of information technology on public administration as well as public policy decision-making
  2. Understand the interaction of four core components of the public sector information environment: policy, management, data, and technology
  3. Synthesize and apply knowledge to discover new opportunities and to manage and eliminate threats of using advanced computer and information technologies in order to solve public policy and management problems.

Technology is important in the public management process because it enhances efficiency, transparency, and value creation. Technological innovation and e-government significantly accelerate public management reforms, leading to improved institutional efficiency and reduced bureaucratic tendencies. Technology tools enable public agencies to achieve internal efficiencies and enhance their operations. The management of public administration must respond to the demands of a globalized world and make use of information and communication systems to achieve strategic objectives. The implementation of public interactive services through information technology can create a healthy business environment and facilitate collaboration between private companies and governments. Computer technology and big data provide a solid foundation for the wisdom of public management, but information sharing barriers need to be addressed for improved quality and level of public management.

Significance of KM and ICT in Public Administration:

Knowledge management and ICT have a significant impact on public administration. The use of KM and technology in public sector organizations can improve efficiency, effectiveness, and service delivery. Traditional knowledge management approaches in the public sector have been criticized for their internal focus and emphasis on data over meanings. However, a social process approach to knowledge formation in public policies can overcome these limitations and provide a better understanding of the role of information and knowledge in decision-making and policy-making. The integration of process management and ICT in public administration can enhance transparency, efficiency, and capacity building. ICTs also play a crucial role in knowledge management processes in the Spanish Public Administration, with success factors identified through real study cases. In the Republic of Slovenia, informatics and informatization are essential for the functioning of public administration, and knowledge management is seen as important for improving service delivery and interconnecting management and informatics.

The use of technological systems, such as e-governance, has the potential to transform the economy and improve governance. Process management combined with ICT can enhance transparency and efficiency in public administration. KM and ICT contribute to greater accessibility and better information and efficiency for citizens, companies, and other entities. Administrative services are developed on the basis of the highest possible level of integration and interoperability between databases and applications.

ICT plays a vital role in supporting powerful, efficient management and administration in the education sector. It is specified that technology can be used right from student administration to various resource administration in an education institution.
